The Ancient Science of Abhyanga

Sunita Rao 28 March 2025 7 min read

Abhyanga — the daily practice of anointing oneself with warm oil — is described in the Ashtanga Hridayam, one of Ayurveda's foundational texts, as one of the most important self-care practices a person can adopt. The word itself comes from Sanskrit: abhi (toward) and anga (limb), pointing to the full-body, intentional nature of the ritual.

Unlike a standard moisturiser applied quickly, Abhyanga is practised slowly and with full attention — typically for 15–20 minutes before bathing. The oil is warmed, and applied in long strokes along the long bones and circular motions at the joints. This movement pattern mirrors the direction of lymphatic flow and venous return.

Modern research in integrative medicine has confirmed several of the benefits described by ancient practitioners. Studies show that regular oil massage reduces salivary cortisol levels, lowers blood pressure, and significantly improves perceived stress within three weeks of daily practice. The lipid layer of the skin is also reinforced, reducing transepidermal water loss — the primary driver of dry, sensitised skin.

At Serenity Spa, our therapists incorporate Abhyanga principles into every treatment. If you'd like to build a home practice, sesame oil is traditionally recommended for Vata types, coconut oil for Pitta, and mustard oil for Kapha. The oil should be warm — not hot — and the practice ideally done in the morning before showering.

"Give yourself an oil massage daily. It will make you strong, give you a good complexion, and nourish your tissues." — Charaka Samhita

The Truth About Double Cleansing

Meera Iyer 14 March 2025 5 min read

Double cleansing originated in Japan, where it has been a cornerstone of skincare routines for decades. The method uses an oil-based cleanser first to dissolve makeup, sunscreen, and sebum — then a water-based cleanser to remove the remaining impurities and balance the skin's pH.

In Bengaluru's climate, where humidity and pollution vary dramatically between seasons, your skin barrier is under constant challenge. During the monsoon months, a lighter oil like jojoba works well as the first cleanse. In the dry winter months, richer oils like marula or rosehip are more appropriate.

The most important thing to understand is that over-cleansing is equally damaging as under-cleansing. If you're double cleansing and still feel tight or dry after, your second cleanser may be too stripping. Switch to a cream or gel formula with a neutral pH (around 5.5) and watch how your skin transforms within two weeks.

5 Breathing Exercises Our Therapists Swear By

Priya Nair 5 March 2025 4 min read

Before stepping into a treatment room, every Serenity therapist spends three minutes with their breath. This isn't superstition — it's a deliberate practice to shift the nervous system from sympathetic (fight-or-flight) to parasympathetic (rest-and-restore) mode. The science is clear: the way you breathe directly governs your physiological state.

1. Box Breathing (4-4-4-4): Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4, exhale for 4, hold for 4. Used by Navy SEALs and yogis alike for equal-minded calm.

2. 4-7-8 Breathing: Inhale 4 counts, hold 7, exhale 8. The extended exhale activates the vagus nerve, dropping heart rate within two cycles.

3. Nadi Shodhana (Alternate Nostril): Close the right nostril, inhale left, switch, exhale right. Repeat. Balances the two hemispheres of the brain and is particularly effective for anxiety.

4. Bhramari (Humming Bee Breath): Exhale while making a gentle humming sound. The vibration stimulates the vagus nerve directly through the vocal cords.

5. Physiological Sigh: Take a full inhale, then a short extra inhale on top, then a long slow exhale. This double inhale pops collapsed alveoli in the lungs and is the fastest way to reduce acute stress.

Understanding Your Dosha

Sunita Rao 20 Feb 2025 8 min read

Ayurveda recognises three fundamental constitutional types — Vata, Pitta, and Kapha — each governed by a combination of the five classical elements (earth, water, fire, air, ether). Most people are a combination of two doshas, with one typically dominant.

Vata (Air + Ether): Creative, quick-thinking, enthusiastic — but prone to anxiety, dry skin, and irregular digestion when imbalanced. Best treatments: warm, grounding, slow. Oils: sesame, almond.

Pitta (Fire + Water): Focused, driven, articulate — but prone to inflammation, redness, and irritability when excess. Best treatments: cooling, calming, medium pressure. Oils: coconut, sunflower.

Kapha (Earth + Water): Steady, nurturing, patient — but prone to sluggishness, weight gain, and congestion when imbalanced. Best treatments: invigorating, deep, stimulating. Oils: mustard, sesame with ginger.

At Serenity, our consultation begins with a simple dosha assessment. This informs oil selection, pressure level, treatment duration, and even the room temperature — so every session is truly bespoke.

Eating for Your Skin: 7 Foods Our Aestheticians Eat Daily

Arjun Das 8 Feb 2025 6 min read

The skin is the body's largest organ, and it reflects everything happening internally. Here are seven foods our team incorporates daily for sustained skin radiance — no expensive supplements required.

1. Amla (Indian Gooseberry): One of the world's densest sources of Vitamin C — 20× more than an orange. Vitamin C is the rate-limiting nutrient for collagen synthesis.

2. Ghee: Rich in butyrate and fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, and K. Supports gut lining integrity, which directly impacts skin barrier function.

3. Turmeric: Curcumin is a potent anti-inflammatory and mild photoprotective agent. Most effective when consumed with black pepper and a fat source.

4. Walnuts: The only nut with a meaningful omega-3 to omega-6 ratio, reducing systemic inflammation that drives acne, eczema, and premature ageing.

5. Papaya: Contains papain, a proteolytic enzyme that gently exfoliates the skin from within and supports healthy digestion.

6. Moringa: Contains all nine essential amino acids — necessary for collagen, elastin, and keratin production.

7. Saffron: Used topically in our Himalayan Ritual and internally for its crocin antioxidants, which protect skin cells from UV-induced damage.

The Sleep Ritual: A 20-Minute Wind-Down

Rahul Sharma 25 Jan 2025 5 min read

Deep sleep is when the body repairs tissue, regulates hormones, and consolidates memory. Yet most adults report that they can't "switch off" at night — lying in bed with an active mind despite physical tiredness. The issue isn't usually sleep itself; it's the transition into sleep.

Our recommended 20-minute ritual:

Minutes 1–5: Sensory cool-down. Lower room temperature to 19–21°C. This triggers melatonin release. Dim all lights to below 10 lux. Avoid screens entirely.

Minutes 5–10: Abhyanga (light version). Apply a small amount of warm sesame or coconut oil to your feet, calves, and hands. The feet have 72,000 nerve endings connected to the entire nervous system — massaging them for five minutes genuinely calms the whole body.

Minutes 10–15: Breathing. Practice the 4-7-8 breathing technique described in our previous article. Six rounds is typically enough to shift into parasympathetic dominance.

Minutes 15–20: Gratitude journaling. Write three specific things that went well today and why. Research by Martin Seligman shows this single practice, done consistently, adds 30–90 minutes of measurable deep sleep within two weeks.

Gua Sha vs. Jade Roller: What the Science Actually Says

Meera Iyer 12 Jan 2025 6 min read

Both tools have become fixtures on every aesthete's vanity. But they do fundamentally different things — and neither does what most influencers claim.

Jade Roller: The rolling motion stimulates lymphatic drainage in the face, reducing morning puffiness. Studies confirm a temporary reduction in under-eye fluid retention. What it cannot do: lift muscles, reduce wrinkles, or change skin texture. Use it cold, in upward and outward strokes, after applying a serum while the skin is still damp.

Gua Sha: Derived from traditional Chinese medicine, gua sha uses scraping pressure to increase microcirculation by up to 400% in the treated area. This brings fresh blood to the surface, temporarily brightening the complexion and reducing fascial tension in the face. Used consistently over months, it can subtly improve facial muscle tone — the only non-invasive tool with credible evidence for this claim.

The winner? Gua sha, for most goals beyond puffiness reduction. But the technique matters enormously — incorrect pressure can break capillaries. Our aestheticians are happy to show you proper technique during any facial treatment.
